A Song of Ice and Fire vs. Lord of the Rings

I am comparing

A Song of Ice and Fire (ASOIAF)

, better known by its TV series adaptation,

Game of Thrones

, by George R. R. Martin to the

Lord of the Rings (LOTR)

trilogy by J. R. R. Tolkien. I chose these series as they are both some of the most iconic and well read books within the fantasy fiction genre. Both series have produced extremely lucrative film/TV adaptations. The LOTR trilogy was published between 1954 and 1955, and the first book in ASOIAF was released in 1996, and it's most recent (5th) book was release in 2011; the series is still being written today. LOTR has a word count of 481,103 words and ASOIAF currently has more than 1.7 million words.

3-Gram Comparison

3-Gram Analysis (top 15)

Despite ASOIAF's massive word count advantage, the freqeuency of the top 15 3-grams is fairly comparable between the two series. LOTR seems to be 1/2 to 1/3 of the frequency of the ASIOAF 3-grams at the same rank.

The top ASOIAF 3-grams seem to be more specific to the series than the LOTR grams. For example, GOT has grams like:

  • king s landing
  • the king s
  • the night s
  • the seven kingdoms

Whereas the only real series specific 3-gram for LOTR is:

  • in the shire

They share 3 grams in common:

  • he did not
  • i don t
  • i do not

Comparing Shared Grams

Shared Gram Analysis

By the nature and commonality of the phrases, it is unsurprising that all 3 grams have similar following words.

While "I don't" and "I do not" are synonymous, it looks like they are not actually used synonymously when written (or "spoken"). For both texts, "I do not" is consistently followed by "know." However, in ASOIAF, "I don't" is more commonly followed by "want" whereas it is still followed by "know" in LOTR.

Contrarily, "he did not" is followed by "know in ASOIAF, but maybe be more commonly followed by "know" or "speak" in LOTR.

Regarding what is (not) known by the characters of both books, the language paints the LOTR characters more of a pondering light, often contemplating within an inner monologue. In ASOIAF, most statements are spoken aloud, with an action-based implication.

Voyant Tools Comparison

Voyant Tools Analysis

Within Voyant Tools, many of the common words are names of the characters for both series. Tyrion, Jon, Arya, etc. vs Frodo, Gandalf, Pippin, etc.

ASOIAF is further inundated by the many titles, such as ser, lord, queen. The use of names and titles appropriately suggests the abundance of characters within the series.

Many of the action-verbs are proactive "came, make, say, took." And several of the more miscellaneous have a darker and more violent nature, for example, "blood, sword, dead,"

On the other hand, many of the LOTR words are descriptive or imply observance.

There are many positional words or adjectives "side, behind, left, far." The action verbs are more passive, "saw, looked, think"

There differences in the two texts aligns with Tolkien's descriptive style that paints the narrative and world to his readers. His characters are thinking and observing the world around them constantly. Martin's style is more plot driven, and is written by new events constantly occuring to keep his reader on edge.
